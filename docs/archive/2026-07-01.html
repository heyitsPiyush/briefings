Hardware Hacking

deep

The past few months have seen significant activity in hardware hacking and embedded security, particularly concerning firmware vulnerabilities and supply chain attacks. A notable incident involved the "FortiBleed" campaign in June, which potentially compromised approximately 80,000 Fortinet firewall/VPN devices globally since February 2026, leveraging credential reuse and brute-force attacks against devices lacking multi-factor authentication. Furthermore, a buffer overflow vulnerability, CVE-2026-25277, was identified in Qualcomm's Strongbox component, affecting various Snapdragon mobile platforms, FastConnect Wi-Fi chips, modem subsystems, audio codecs, and XR platforms, allowing local authenticated attackers to corrupt memory. Another firmware flaw, CVE-2026-56111, in Marlin Firmware through version 2.1.2.7, allows out-of-bounds writes via the M421 G-code handler when MESHBEDLEVELING is enabled, potentially leading to denial of service or firmware state corruption.

Supply chain security remains a critical concern, with physical tampering and hardware backdoors at the supplier level making headlines, including instances of hardware implants in supply chains serving commercial and military targets. The "Atomic Arch" campaign, disclosed between June 9 and June 12, 2026, involved backdooring over 1,500 packages in the Arch User Repository by exploiting its orphan adoption workflow to inject malicious build logic. Researchers also presented InjectV, a new fault injection framework for RISC-V platforms built on the gem5 simulator, enabling precise, guided fault injection at security-critical execution points to systematically explore attack vectors. New hardware hacking tools gaining traction include the M5 Monster C5, built around the ESP32-C5 chip, offering dual-band Wi-Fi 6 capabilities for multi-channel deauthentication and WPA3 attacks.

AI × Cybersecurity

deep

The intersection of AI and cybersecurity is rapidly evolving, with a significant focus on Large Language Model (LLM) vulnerabilities and AI-powered attacks. Prompt injection remains a critical concern, identified as the top vulnerability by OWASP (LLM01:2025), with researchers like Charles Ye, Jasmine Cui, and MIT's Dylan Hadfield-Menell demonstrating "CoT Forgery" techniques that trick models such as OpenAI's GPT-OSS-20B. Additionally, AI-generated browser ransomware, created using models like DeepSeek, has emerged, leveraging novel attack paths that combine browser-malware concepts with legitimate browser capabilities to execute ransomware entirely within the browser on Windows and Android devices. The 2026 Bitdefender Cybersecurity Assessment highlights self-mutating malware, public LLM data leakage, and AI-driven evasion techniques as top AI-related threats, while AI is also accelerating existing attack methods like phishing and social engineering.

To counter these threats, defensive AI tools and practices are becoming increasingly vital. Organizations are adopting AI red teaming, a systematic adversarial testing approach for LLMs and AI agents, to identify vulnerabilities before exploitation. Companies like Forward AI are recognized for their secure autonomous network operations, while a range of defensive AI tools are available, including Google Cloud Security AI Workbench for cloud forensics, SentinelSphere for real-time agent threat detection, and Microsoft PyRIT for prompt red-teaming. Research into securing AI pipelines and RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) systems emphasizes robust security protocols, including encryption, zero-trust architectures, and guardrails like immutable system prompts and input sanitization to prevent prompt injection and unauthorized data access. The Five Eyes cyber security agencies also urge leaders to use AI to strengthen defense, emphasizing foundational cybersecurity practices and continuous adaptation to evolving threats.
