AI × Cybersecurity

deep

The intersection of AI and cybersecurity in early 2026 highlights a rapidly evolving threat landscape where AI empowers both attackers and defenders. Cybercriminals are increasingly using AI-as-a-service platforms and models like WormGPT and FraudGPT to automate vulnerability scans, generate convincing phishing emails, create self-modifying malware, and deploy deepfakes for impersonation, making attacks smarter and more accessible. Notable AI-driven attacks include AI-driven ransomware, automated phishing, and adversarial AI attacks such as prompt injection and data poisoning. Organizations like PwC are reporting a surge in identity-centric attacks, where adversaries exploit legitimate access rather than breaching perimeters, often augmented by AI-enabled social engineering and multi-stage phishing campaigns.

Research into LLM vulnerabilities, particularly prompt injection, remains a critical focus, with organizations like Witness AI noting it as the number one vulnerability on the OWASP Top 10 for LLM Applications 2025. Prompt injection attacks, which manipulate LLMs by inserting malicious instructions to override original system prompts, are evolving beyond simple overrides to exploit how models merge information from multiple sources, including retrieved documents and tool responses. To counter these threats, defensive AI tools and practices are advancing, with companies such as Anthropic launching initiatives like Project Glasswing to apply AI to defensive cybersecurity, actively hunting vulnerabilities and reinforcing system strength. AI red teaming tools are also becoming crucial, with platforms like Repello AI ARTEMIS and open-source frameworks such as Microsoft's PyRIT, Nvidia's Garak, and Giskard, offering comprehensive coverage for RAG pipelines, agentic systems, and the full AI attack surface, including prompt injection and data leakage detection.
