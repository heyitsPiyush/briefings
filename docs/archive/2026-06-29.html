Hardware Hacking

deep

Hardware hacking and embedded security news as of June 29, 2026, reveals significant developments across various attack vectors. A notable hardware vulnerability, dubbed "usbliter8," has been disclosed, enabling arbitrary code execution within the SecureROM of Apple's A12 and A13 chips. This unpatchable exploit requires physical access to devices in DFU mode, utilizing a dedicated RP2350-based microcontroller board to bypass Apple's signed boot chain in under two seconds. The flaw stems from the Synopsys DWC2 USB controller's handling of direct memory access on affected Apple A12 and A13 devices, allowing unintended writes to critical system memory.

Furthermore, the embedded security landscape is heavily impacted by firmware vulnerabilities and IoT security concerns. Over 21,500 Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures (CVEs) related to IoT firmware were disclosed in the first half of 2026, with Remote Code Execution (RCE) and authentication bypasses remaining critical threats. In March 2026, law enforcement dismantled four IoT botnets, Aisuru, Kimwolf, JackSkid, and Mossad, which had infected over 3 million devices globally, including routers, web cameras, and smart TVs, and were capable of generating DDoS attacks exceeding 31 Tbps. Supply chain security also remains a critical focus, with hardware and firmware insertion attacks targeting boot chains and management controllers, alongside concerns about the compromise of AI/machine learning model pipelines. Russia unveiled its LFI-26 laser fault injection system, designed to identify hidden vulnerabilities in microchips across various devices, including banking cards, cryptocurrency wallets, smartphones, and automotive control units.

AI × Cybersecurity

deep

The intersection of AI and cybersecurity is rapidly evolving, with significant developments observed in June 2026 across various fronts. Large Language Models (LLMs) continue to present vulnerabilities, particularly with prompt injection remaining a critical and largely unsolved problem, as highlighted by Ariel Fogel of OWASP. Research indicates that LLMs struggle to differentiate between developer instructions and user-supplied content, leading to attacks like those reported by CrowdStrike, which saw prompt injection used to steal credentials and cryptocurrency from over 90 organizations in 2025. Furthermore, indirect prompt injection, where malicious instructions are embedded in external data, poses a growing threat, exemplified by the "EchoLeak" vulnerability against Microsoft 365 Copilot, enabling data exfiltration via crafted emails. The rapid adoption of AI is also accelerating AI-powered attacks, with CrowdStrike reporting an 89% year-over-year increase in AI-enabled adversary operations in 2025, often involving no traditional malicious code.

In response to these escalating threats, defensive AI tools are gaining traction, with organizations increasingly implementing AI-enabled tools for phishing detection, intrusion detection, and user behavior analytics. Companies like OpenAI and Anthropic are actively engaging in AI red teaming to identify vulnerabilities in their frontier models, such as Anthropic's Claude Mythos and OpenAI's GPT-5.6 Sol, which have reportedly uncovered thousands of critical software flaws. Governments, including the U.S. with President Trump's June 2026 Executive Order, are also pushing for enhanced cyber defenses through initiatives like an "AI cybersecurity clearinghouse" to coordinate vulnerability information and patch distribution. Securing AI pipelines and Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems is a growing area of research, as these systems introduce new attack surfaces, including data leakage from vector databases and the risk of prompt injection at the retrieval layer, which current IAM and data controls are not adequately designed to govern.
