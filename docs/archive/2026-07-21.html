Hardware Hacking

deep

The embedded security market is experiencing significant growth, driven by the proliferation of IoT devices and increasing cyber threats, with projections reaching US$8.9 billion in 2026. Hardware-rooted security solutions, such as secure elements, Trusted Platform Modules (TPMs), and Hardware Security Modules (HSMs), are seeing increased demand across IoT gateways, automotive systems, and payment devices. Notably, a recent vulnerability, CVE-2026-38754, in BusyBox 1.38.0's ash shell, was discovered in July 2026, which can cause a heap-buffer overflow via crafted input, primarily leading to denial of service in various embedded products and Linux environments. Another critical firmware vulnerability, CVE-2026-48111, an off-by-one out-of-bounds read in 7-Zip versions 9.21 through 26.00, affects UEFI firmware parsing.

Research in side-channel and fault injection attacks continues to advance, with new tools like InjectV targeting RISC-V platforms within the gem5 simulator to identify security weaknesses through transient fault attacks in registers and memory. Furthermore, side-channel attack mitigation for quantum-resistant cryptographic implementations is a pressing concern, as even quantum-proof algorithms can leak secret keys through electromagnetic radiation from chips during processing, as demonstrated by research on GPU side-channels extracting weights from NVIDIA Jetson chips. Supply chain security remains a critical area, with the hardware supply chain facing fragility due to component shortages and geopolitical tensions, making traceability and inspections crucial. The rise of interconnected IoT ecosystems and AI-driven automation further expands the attack surface, emphasizing the need to secure not just individual devices but also the broader trust relationships and infrastructure.
